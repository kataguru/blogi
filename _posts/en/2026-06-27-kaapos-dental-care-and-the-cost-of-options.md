<p class="meta">27.6.2026 · Seinäjoki</p>

Kaapo just got a painkiller.

Five teeth were removed from it yesterday. Kaapo is a mittelspitz, a small dog, but small size does not make responsibility cheap.

Dental care for dogs is expensive. The price of this operation was 840 euros. For this year, dental care is estimated to accumulate a bill of at least 1,600 euros.

This can be seen as an expense, and it is. But it's not just an expense. It is the price that the animal does not have to live with pain because a person saves in the wrong place.

At this point, the question is ultimately not: is this expensive?

The question is: what options do you really have?

If the dog is your responsibility, its pain is not a matter of negotiation. Money makes it difficult. That doesn't make it optional.

## Why are Kaapo's teeth in such bad shape?

That's a good question.

The first doubt naturally falls on the person. Have we been bad dog owners? Have we given the wrong treats, too soft food or too little dental care? Have we failed to do something when we should have done something?

Probably partly the reason lies with us as well. There are rarely completely clean explanations.

But this still doesn't explain everything.

Olivia is also a Mittelspitz. He is two years older than Kaapo, and he does not have the same dental problems. The same house, the same everyday life, the same basic logic of eating, the same people. Still, the end result is different.

That's why the question changes.

It's not just about how the dog has been cared for. It's also about what kind of structure it got when it was born.

In the case of Kaapo, the hereditary factors seem to be bad. The gums recede, and the enamel does not hold normally. As I recall, the term used by the vet was enamel hypoplasia or some related enamel development disorder.

This does not eliminate liability. That narrows it down.

If the system is already weak from its initial settings, it cannot be managed in the same way as a strong system. It requires more monitoring, more money, and more decisions that wouldn't be made if everything were business as usual.

So Kaapo's teeth are not in bad shape just because something was neglected. They are in poor condition because part of the system is structurally weak.

It's unfair to the dog. And expensive for a person.

But still the same question remains:

what options do you really have?
