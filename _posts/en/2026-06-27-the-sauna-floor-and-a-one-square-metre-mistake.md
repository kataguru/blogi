<p class="meta">27.6.2026 · Evijärvi</p>

The picture on the front page shows a sauna.

It looks old, even though it's still a work in progress. The reason is iron wind. The treatment turns the wood gray quickly and gives the new building an old surface. So the exterior looks more finished and older than the structure actually is.

Inside, the work has progressed further than you can see from the picture. The floor is thermally insulated. The space formed by the sauna and the washroom has tiling on top of lightweight concrete casting. Lightweight concrete is poured on top of the floorboards.

Then comes the point where reality gives feedback.

The combination of haste, fatigue and age meant that the inclines didn't quite go to the finish line. In connection with the first Midsummer sauna, I noticed that the water was standing in a couple of places.

It's a small mistake on paper. In practice, it's exactly the kind of mistake that shouldn't be explained away. Standing water in a wet state is not a matter of opinion. It's feedback.

We considered the options. You could put a slatted mat on the floor. After bathing, the water could be removed with a spatula. Both solutions would work on some level.

But they wouldn't fix the problem.

They would mask the symptom or make it a hassle to repeat the treatment procedure. After every sauna, someone would remember that this point was wrong. Every stroke of the trowel would be a small reminder that the root cause was left under the floor.

The right way is to get to the root cause.

The same structure can be seen elsewhere.

Modern medicine too often resembles using a trowel on an improperly sloped floor. One extra layer is added: pill, control, treatment period, new measurement. The symptom can be temporarily controlled in the same way as standing water can be temporarily removed with a spatula.

But the floor is still on the wrong slope.

This does not mean that the drugs are useless. They are necessary in many situations. The same applies to the trowel: yes, it gets the water out. The problem arises when managing the symptom becomes a permanent substitute for fixing the root cause.

Why does this happen?

There are many reasons. The system is busy. A person often seeks treatment only when the problem is already on. Doctors are trained more to fight fires than to make long-term adjustments to everyday structures. And the patient often resists change. It is easier to take a tablet than to change sleep, food, movement, alcohol, smoking or weight. A boring doctor says out loud what a person already knows but doesn't want to hear.

Still, behind everything there is also money.

A cured client does not produce in the same way as a client who is continuously cared for. A system that corrects the root cause reduces its own billing. A system that treats the symptom will keep the customer coming back.

This is a strong claim and does not explain everything. But it does explain part of why a child is so often easier to accept than tearing up the floor.

Repairing the structure is laborious, slow and inconvenient. Treating the symptom is faster, cleaner and easier to sell.

The next day, I tore the tiles off the problem areas before they were completely dry. Today I started fixing the floor. The area to be repaired is approximately one square meter.

This is the construction operating system in practice.

A mistake does not go away by calling it harmful. It disappears only by fixing the structure. First, we disassemble as much as we have to. Then the tilt is done again. Then we will see if the behavior of the system changed.

One square doesn't sound like much.

But sometimes just that one square tells you where the rush, load and reality no longer met.

## Why is a log sauna a good example?

Let's go back to the log sauna.

Why is this a good example? Because the structure itself does a large part of the work.

The log now practically requires no maintenance at all. No painting, no scratching, no constant renewal of chemical layers. Iron weathering makes the surface gray, but does not build maintenance slavery. The surface is allowed to age without having to constantly save it.

Kivijala is high and ventilated. The structure does not lie against moisture, and it does not have to fight against moisture rising from the ground. As the air circulates, the structure stays drier. When the texture stays drier, it doesn't rot as much.

The eaves are long. They protect the log from direct wetting. This is a simple thing, but that's why it's important. A good structure does not need constant aftercare if it is properly protected in the first place.

In practice, the sauna is ecological, low-maintenance and long-lasting.

It is not ecological because a green explanation is pasted on it. It is ecological because it has a long service life, requires little maintenance and the materials are understandable. Sustainability is not born from a slogan. It arises from a structure that does not require constant repair to stay alive.

In this sense, a log sauna is the opposite of treating symptoms.

When the basic structure is right, later life doesn't have to be about constant patching. The same applies to the house, health, work and everyday life. If the structure protects itself, man is freed to sustain life and not just fail.

I believe that the next generation will be able to enjoy this sauna as well.

That's a good metric. Not whether the building looks new in the first summer. But whether it is still in use when the first factors have already receded.
