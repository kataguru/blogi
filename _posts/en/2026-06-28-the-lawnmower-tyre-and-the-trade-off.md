<p class="meta">28.6.2026 · Evijärvi</p>

Evijärvi cottage has a cheap ride-on lawnmower.

Cheap does not mean bad here. It means that the machine will do its job as long as you accept its limitations.

Originally, the area to be cut was about 3,000 square meters. That's a lot for a cottage yard. In the end, we decided to let about 800 square meters fallow. It was the first sensible correction: you don't have to maintain everything with the same intensity.

There was still about 2,200 square meters left.

It takes about two hours to drive the entire area with a ride-on mower. It's not an impossible job if you do it once. But over the summer it becomes a recurring maintenance task. Same route, same plane, same sound, same time away from somewhere else.

We considered a robotic mower at the very beginning.

Then a reasonable robotic mower would have been expensive, and the area would have required an insane amount of boundary wire. I wasn't interested in the planting of the border wires, the repair and the idea of buying an expensive system that requires its own infrastructure to replace a cheap cottage mower.

We chose a cheap ride-on mower.

This should not be described as mere saving.

It was a barter agreement.

We did not pay a large amount for automation, positioning and a ready-made cutting system. We paid less money and agreed that part of the price would be paid later in our own time, in fuel, maintenance, spare parts and two-hour mowing rounds.

It was a choice.

Not necessarily the wrong choice. But it's a choice whose real price is only visible in use.

In two years, the situation has changed. Robotic mowers have evolved. Boundary wire is no longer always needed in the same way, when machines know how to use positioning and smarter control. But the model that cuts 2,200 square meters still costs around two thousand euros.

My estimate is that next spring the limit will be exceeded.

When the price drops enough, the robot mower may no longer be a luxury item. It could be a change of maintenance method. You don't just buy a machine, you remove one repetitive work cycle from human responsibility.

Until then, the old vehicle can continue.

One day, however, the cut was horrible. The grass was uneven, and my wife noticed it immediately.

That was a good observation. The sound of the machine may be the same, the driver the same and the route the same, but the trace tells us that something in the system has changed.

We're looking into it.

The rear tire was flat. The tubeless tire had lost its valve.

I still had time to go to the store. I bought a new valve, put it in place and the cutting continued. The repair seemed easy. Small part, small price, small effort.

But the next weekend the tire was empty again.

This time the valve was partially broken.

That's when I decided to change my approach. I would no longer just put a valve, but install an inner tube in the tire. I bought two just to be sure.

The idea made sense on paper.

Inner rubber would bypass the tightness problem of a tubeless tire. The valve would no longer be the only critical point. The old tire could continue to be used without me buying the entire tire package with rims.

But the small ring was not an easy opponent.

Installing the inner tube on the small tubeless tire required a technique I didn't quite know. The tire didn't give in with the logic that bigger and easier things often give in to. I had to learn by hand how to get the tire off the rim, how to get the inner tube in without breaking it and how to put everything back together so that the air stays where it should.

Finally it worked.

But at the same time it became clear that the same exchange agreement was repeated in a smaller size.

I could have bought the whole tire with rims. It would have been a more expensive but faster solution. Instead, I bought the valve first and the inner tube later. The monetary price decreased, but the amount of work increased.

I chose the cheaper part and accepted the difference as my own work.

This does not make the choice wrong.

It makes it visible.

In everyday life, many things look like savings because we only look at one price tag. The valve costs a little. Inner rubber costs a little. The whole tire with rims costs more. Therefore, a cheaper option seems reasonable.

But the real cost of the system is not just the part.

It includes time, tools, interruption, learning curve, risk of failure, another shopping trip and that moment when you should have done something else, but ended up wrestling with a small ring.

A cheap solution is good if it really reduces the overall load.

It's bad when it just shifts the cost from money to time, maintenance and repeated friction.

A lawnmower tire is a small part. But that's exactly why it's a good example. The entire 2,200 square meter cutting system stops at one tire not holding air.

The chain of maintenance is only as strong as its most mundane, cheapest and most easily forgotten part.

The same can be seen elsewhere.

An old machine can be a reasonable solution if its maintenance remains light. But when a cheap machine starts to require constant attention, its price changes. It doesn't appear on the purchase receipt, but on weekends.

Therefore, next spring's robotic mower may not be an expensive toy.

It may be a sign that the way the system is maintained is being changed.

The ride-on mower solved the problem for the first few years. It was good enough and cheap enough. But if every summer starts to consist of two-hour mowing rounds, patching a tire and learning small mistakes, the question changes.

No more asking which part is the cheapest.

We ask which structure releases the most life back into use.
