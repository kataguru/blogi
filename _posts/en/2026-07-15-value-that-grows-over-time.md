Over the years, I've noticed that almost all of my most important decisions come back to the same question:

**Will this increase or decrease in value over time?**

When I was younger, I thought I was looking for bargains. In hindsight, I realize that I wasn't looking for goods, but value.

The joy of discovery has been preserved. It's just changed shape.

Today, a successful investment brings me as much joy as a new car brings to many others. The car's charm is real but short-lived: after a couple of weeks, it becomes just a car. A well-chosen investment, on the other hand, reminds of its existence for years. It's growing. It opens up new possibilities.

The same idea is reflected in what I build.

When designing the sauna, the goal was not the cheapest possible sauna. But I also didn't want a forever project at any cost. I asked two things at once:

- How can this be done at a reasonable price?
- How does this become such that a lifespan of one hundred years is a realistic assumption?

Many consider these goals contradictory. I don't think they are. The best solution is often found between them.

The same goes for hikes. I am more interested in the mountains of Albania, Romania and Montenegro than ready-made experiences. Not because they are cheaper, but because there is more to discover. I enjoy being able to build my own journey.

Maybe I'm still a hunter-gatherer.

I'm still on the hunt for affordable food and goods finds. The only difference is that nowadays I also hunt for things that increase in value over time.

The same way of thinking can be seen in human relationships.

Many are looking for a person who thinks the same way. Trust has become more important to me over the years.

My wife and I disagree on many things. She notices things in the world that I do not. I, in turn, see things that are not as meaningful to her.

Still, we have learned to make joint decisions. Not because the other person wins or because we always end up in a compromise, but because we first try to understand why the other person thinks the way they do.

Sometimes my point of view is chosen, sometimes his.

Taking turns doesn't feel like losing when trust is in order. Knows that the other will not take advantage of the situation. Know that next time your point of view will get the same weight.

I have come to believe that trust is much more important than like-mindedness.

Opinions change. People change. Life situations change.

Confidence can last through them all.

Looking back, the same phenomenon repeats itself in almost everything I hold dear.

Purchased goods lose their novelty.

A self-built sauna gets more stories.

The investment grows interest by interest.

Long hikes turn into memories, the value of which increases every time you think back to them.

A long relationship gets a common history.

Perhaps the most important skill in life is not finding the cheapest or the most expensive.

Perhaps the most important thing is to learn to recognize the few things whose value does not wear out with use but increases over time.
