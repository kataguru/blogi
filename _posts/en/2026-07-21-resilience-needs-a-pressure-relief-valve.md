Resilience is often spoken of as the ability to endure difficulties and recover from them.

The idea is correct, but not enough.

If a person is constantly burdened, the ability to endure does not increase endlessly. At some point, the system starts using more resources to maintain its performance than it can afford.

Then everything can still look normal on the outside.

The work is done. Bills are paid. The family is taken care of. Messages are answered and we are at the agreed places on time.

Still, the system runs at too high a utilization rate all the time.

The collapse appears sudden only because no one saw the load build up.

## Resilience is not just about enduring

I have been interested in allostasis and resilience for a long time.

Allostasis means the body's ability to regulate its activity in anticipation and move its balance point according to the requirements of the situation. The system does not keep everything unchanged, but adapts to the load.

Resilience means the ability to withstand disturbances, recover from them and continue operating.

In addition to these, a guiding layer is needed.

Its task is to anticipate, prioritize, lighten the load and change the environment before the capacity runs out.

But even that alone is not enough.

A person must also have a pressure valve.

## A moment without supervision

It's important for me to get out of the control of others sometimes.

No tracking.

No phone control.

No obligation to tell where I am, what I'm doing or when I'll be back.

You don't have to use your time wisely either.

I can do whatever I want or do nothing.

Such a state is not ordinary rest.

Even resting can be an achievement. You can measure your run, track your sleep quality, optimize your recovery and use your free time efficiently.

Then the person has not left the control system. He's just taken to monitoring himself.

The real pressure valve only opens when its own internal controller does not demand results either.

## The restoration of autonomy

The most plausible explanation is related to autonomy.

When a person is constantly under the expectations, responsibilities and evaluation of others, he must always take into account the possible reactions of others.

What does anyone think?

Should I reply to the message?

Is something expected of me?

Am I using my time correctly?

Such constant anticipation consumes regulatory capacity, even if there is no actual crisis.

When control and expectations are removed for a moment, agency is restored. In work recovery research, the same phenomenon is called psychological disengagement: recovery requires that a person disengages from the load at the level of thought as well, not just physically.

A person again gets the experience that he owns his own time and activities.

It does not remove responsibility from life. It cuts it off for a while.

It is this interruption that seems to strengthen resilience.

## The pressure valve is not an escape

It is important to separate the pressure valve from the escape of responsibility.

A healthy disengagement does not mean that a person disappears without notice, leaves his responsibilities to others, or uses his freedom in harmful activities.

Absence can be agreed upon.

But its content does not need to be reported.

The other person does not need to know every route, activity or thought. Safety can be ensured without constant monitoring.

Autonomy does not mean freedom from responsibility.

It means temporary freedom from control.

## Resilience is also built by removing the load

Developing resilience is easily misunderstood.

We think that you get stronger by increasing the load: by doing harder things, enduring discomfort and pushing yourself a little further.

A suitable load can strengthen, but only when there is enough capacity left for recovery.

A system that is constantly loaded without proper decoupling will not develop stronger. It becomes more fragile.

That's why resilience is not built just by exercising under load.

It is also being built:

* by reducing constant monitoring
* by removing unnecessary duties
* setting aside time that doesn't have a goal
* allowing complete inaccessibility
* by giving people control over their own time

## A surprising collapse is not always a surprise

A person can last a long time. That is exactly why his load is easily misjudged.

A conscientious and capable person will maintain the system for a long time even with too much load. He fills in the gaps, postpones his own needs and moves on.

When the capacity finally runs out, the environment is surprised.

But the system didn't collapse overnight. It wore off gradually. In physiology, this accumulated wear and tear has a name: allostatic load — the price the body pays for constant adaptation.

The function of the pressure valve is to prevent the pressure from rising to a level where a small additional load triggers a large disturbance.

## A strong system also knows how to detach

Resilience does not mean that a person can withstand everything.

A strong system recognizes its limits and switches to recovery mode in time.

It can momentarily close connections, stop reporting and leave the scope of other expectations.

Not because the world is too much.

But because you can return to the world fully functional.

Regulates your platform.

Resilience restores.

The control layer anticipates.

The pressure valve prevents the entire system from breaking.
