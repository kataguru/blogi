The current relationship market sometimes resembles a poorly designed mobile application.

The user interface is finished, the images are optimized and the finger swipes the screen almost automatically. Each new hit yields a small reward and the promise that the next option might be even slightly better.

However, an endless loop runs in the background.

Bandwidth is used up, the processor heats up and new connections are constantly being made, but few of them ever make it into a working system.

We have learned to quickly find a compatible person. At the same time, we may have forgotten how to learn to reconcile two different people.

## User interface bias

The business of dating apps is based on active users. A person who finds a permanent partner and deletes the application is no longer a customer of the service.

Therefore, the system constantly offers new options. It easily creates the impression that a slightly prettier, more interesting and more compatible person is waiting behind the next swipe.

This results in a *fail-fast* culture of human relationships.

When the first friction occurs, the connection can be severed before either has time to understand where the problem arose. It is easier to start a new conversation than to learn to deal with the other person's fears, needs, habits and traces left by the past.

At the same time, the connection is easily left half-hearted.

Even if two people are physically in the same place, the search for a better alternative can still be running in the background. You don't dare to allocate resources to the current relationship, because the choice might turn out to be wrong.

But a system that is never committed to cannot be properly tested either.

## The demo version is not a production environment

The beginning of the relationship usually takes place in a controlled environment.

Clothes are chosen carefully, messages are thought out in advance and meetings are organized when both have time and energy. Each shows a self-optimized demo version.

There's nothing wrong with that. It is rare to start a new acquaintance by presenting your worst sides first.

However, the demo version tells only a little about the actual stability of the system.

Compatibility only starts to show in ordinary life:

- What happens when the other person is tired, sick or worried?
- How is the disagreement resolved?
- Can the other admit his mistake?
- Are responsibilities shared without constant negotiation?
- Can both be weak sometimes?
- Will the connection be restored after the conflict?

The real stress test of a relationship is not a deliberately arranged fight or a simulated crisis. Life produces enough load without artificial testing.

A joint trip, renovation, illness, money worries, a supervised night or a difficult family situation quickly reveal more than ten successful restaurant evenings.

Then we will see if the system shares the load or if one transfers all the responsibility to the other.

## Log files cannot be hidden indefinitely

In the early stages of the relationship, both filter information.

You don't have to tell everything at the first meeting. Trust is built gradually, and it wouldn't even make sense to completely open up your privacy to a stranger.

At some point, however, the relationship has to move from the presentation layer to real information.

Before making big decisions, it would be good to at least know what kind of system you are joining.

How does the other person relate to money, health, loyalty, children, work, responsibility and drugs? What kind of commitments, illnesses, debts or unfinished relationships from the past follow along?

This does not mean that a complete audit report should be drawn up on the person.

The purpose is to prevent a situation where the fundamental risks of the relationship are revealed only after the home, finances and life have already been built on top of them.

Honesty should also be two-way.

The information is not disclosed to the interrogator, who himself remains protected. Both of them have to gradually open their own logs: their weaknesses, fears, limitations and those aspects that they don't like to present in the user interface.

Trust is born from the fact that the other person sees the flaws in the system and does not use them as a weapon later.

## There is no complete version

As a software tester, I know that there is no such thing as a completely flawless system.

The same goes for people.

We all have old code, unfixed bugs and workarounds created over the years. Some work surprisingly well. Some break under load. Some features will never change again.

From the point of view of a relationship, it is not the absence of mistakes that is decisive, but what kind of mistakes there are in the system and how they are handled.

Not all flaws need to be accepted. Violence, constant lying, addictions or systematically violating the other person's boundaries are not harmless characteristics that you just have to learn to live with.

But a lot of people's faults are ordinary incompleteness: impatience, withdrawal, insecurity, rigid ways, and difficulty speaking about one's needs.

When these traits are known before major commitments, accepting them is a conscious decision.

Then the problem that comes up later is not a completely unexpected system error. It is a known limitation that has been decided to live with and that can be tried to be corrected together.

## Commitment is an accepted risk

A lasting relationship does not come from two perfectly compatible people finding each other.

More likely, it arises from two sufficiently compatible people understanding what they are getting into.

They have seen more than just the user interface in each other. They've experienced the usual load, resolved a few real conflicts, and disclosed enough of their own log data.

After that they make a decision.

Not because there couldn't be a better alternative in the world, but because it's worth building the system with this person.

Commitment is not a guarantee of a flawless future.

It's a decision to stop constantly scanning for options and use resources for the connection that's already been made.

That, if anything, is sustainable architecture.
