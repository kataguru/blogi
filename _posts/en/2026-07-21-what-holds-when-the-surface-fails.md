I've written about our slippery stairs before.

The stairs looked neat, so I thought they were fine. My wife noticed something more important: the dog was afraid to climb them.

We looked at the same stairs, but asked different questions. I asked what they looked like. She asked whether they worked.

The difference sounds self-evident. Yet it is easily overlooked because the surface is the first thing we see. A solution that looks good seems to work. The convincing reasoning feels true. The familiar way seems reasonable.

At least until it is tested.

## Ideas need pressure-testing

I recently spent an evening talking to an artificial intelligence. In the end, the topic of conversation was not very important. What was more interesting was what I found myself doing: I pressed each thought to see if it would hold.

I don't discuss just to defend my opinion. I say something, look for the point where the argument starts to falter, and refine it. Sometimes the argument holds up. Sometimes only a small part of it remains. Sometimes it breaks down completely.

Software works the same way. You do not understand a program merely by looking at it. You test it, place it under load and use it in ways its designer did not expect. Only then do its limits become visible.

Thoughts should be treated with equal suspicion.

A good argument and a good-sounding argument may seem similar at first. The difference only becomes apparent when pressure is applied to them. The other withstands counter-questions. The other still needs new words to support it.

So you can't always tell the surface from the core by looking. Sometimes you have to push it.

## What is worth remembering about me?

During the same conversation, I reviewed the information that the AI tool had stored about me in its memory.

There were mentions of the tools I used, the projects I built, and the ways I had once worked. Much of the information had been true at the time. Now they were out of date.

The tools had changed. The projects had ended. Some details that seemed important no longer told me anything at all.

When the outdated data was removed, there was surprisingly little left. However, that bit was more meaningful: My way of putting things together, accepting incompleteness as part of what I do, and being careful about what information is stored about me.

Individual projects changed, but the way to do them remained.

In that moment I understood that slippage is not always a fault. I like to move from one tool and project to another. I don't want to commit to one medium just because it's familiar. Projects should change.

Slippage on the steps was a problem I hadn't noticed. In my work, it can be a conscious choice.

The difference is whether I have chosen a compromise and understood its consequences. The chosen compromise can be a good design decision. An inadvertent compromise is often just a fault for which we have come up with an explanation.

## Flexibility is not yet wisdom

Separating the surface and the core requires a willingness to change one's mind. It's difficult, because opinions easily become a part of identity. When a statement turns out to be wrong, it feels as if something in ourselves is wrong.

Changing your mind is not enough on its own. If you bend with every persuasive speaker, you are not open-minded but easily led.

That's why we need two abilities at the same time: the readiness to give up bad ideas and the ability to recognize when the justification offered in their place is really better.

A beautiful speech is not the same as a good argument. Confidence is not the same as certainty. And a pleasant answer is not necessarily true.

## The test also has an expiration date

An approved test says that the thing worked at the time, under the conditions and with the data it was tested on. It does not promise that the result will remain true forever.

The terms of the insurance may expire in relation to the life situation. An electricity contract that made sense when it was signed can turn out to be expensive. Even in a relationship, the expectations agreed upon years ago can no longer match what people need today. The same applies to jobs, service contracts and our own ways.

The contract may therefore still be valid but still expired. That's why important contracts have to be put on the table from time to time and asked again: does this still correspond to reality?

Our assessments of ourselves also grow old. What was the right solution for me last year may not be right now. Past success means that something worked once. It does not prove that the same solution will work next time.

Lottery history does not guarantee future numbers.

This does not make the previous assessment wrong. That makes it temporal. True then does not necessarily mean true now.

## Checking never stops

Tools, projects, beliefs, agreements and our perception of ourselves change. It is not an exception but a normal state.

That's why you shouldn't try to keep everything the same. It is more important to learn to recognize what endures through change — and when something that endured before needs to be tested again.

I didn't remember one big answer from the evening's conversation. What remained was a better way to ask:

- What is just the surface here?
- What happens if you try this?
- When was this last checked?
- What remains when the obsolete is removed?

The slippery stairs reminded us that a clean surface does not guarantee functionality. Outdated memory showed that even accurate information does not necessarily tell the essentials. The contracts, on the other hand, remind us that an estimate made once does not automatically remain correct.

The terrain is constantly moving. That's why a ready-made map is never enough.

We need a way to check it again — and an understanding that each response has its own time.
